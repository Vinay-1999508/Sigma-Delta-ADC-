#  Analog Front-End: 2nd-Order Sigma-Delta Modulator

This directory contains the continuous-time analog circuit design and simulation files for the mixed-signal pipeline. The circuit was designed, tuned, and verified using **LTspice**.

The primary function of this stage is to sample a $1\text{ kHz}$ analog sine wave and modulate it into a high-speed $10\text{ MHz}$ 1-bit Pulse-Density Modulated (PDM) bitstream using a 2nd-order feedback loop.

---

##  Circuit Architecture

A 2nd-order continuous-time Sigma-Delta ($\Sigma\Delta$) modulator consists of three main stages:
1. **Two Cascaded Integrators (The "Sigma"):** Built using operational amplifiers with capacitor feedback. These act as low-pass filters for the input signal and high-pass filters for the quantization noise.
2. **A 1-Bit Quantizer (The "Delta"):** A high-speed comparator clocked at $10\text{ MHz}$ that acts as a 1-bit Analog-to-Digital Converter (ADC). It compares the output of the second integrator to a reference voltage and outputs either a logic HIGH or LOW.
3. **A 1-Bit DAC Feedback Loop:** The digital output is fed back into the summing nodes of the integrators. This negative feedback constantly attempts to keep the integrated error at zero.

### Why 2nd-Order?
A 1st-order modulator shapes quantization noise at $+20\text{ dB/decade}$. By adding a second integrator into the loop, we achieve **$+40\text{ dB/decade}$ noise shaping**. This aggressively pushes almost all the quantization noise generated by the comparator out of the audio baseband and into the high-frequency spectrum, allowing for extreme resolution after digital filtering.

---

##  Engineering Challenges & Solutions

### The Challenge: Op-Amp Integrator Saturation & DC Biasing
The most critical challenge in designing a continuous-time $\Sigma\Delta$ modulator is keeping the op-amps in their linear operating region. Because integrators accumulate error over time, any slight DC offset in the input signal or slight mismatch in the feedback loop will cause the integrator voltages to drift continuously until the op-amps hit their supply rails (saturation).

### The Solution
1. **Careful DC Biasing:** A precise common-mode DC bias voltage was established for the non-inverting inputs of the op-amps to ensure the AC signal swings symmetrically between the supply rails.
2. **Feedback Scaling:** The resistor values in the DAC feedback path were carefully tuned to ensure the negative feedback injects just enough charge to bound the integrator states, pulling them back toward the center point before they can clip against the voltage rails.

##  Simulation Specifications

To generate the data for the digital back-end, a strict transient simulation was executed.

* **Input Signal ($V_{in}$):** $1\text{ kHz}$ continuous sine wave.
* **Sampling Clock ($f_{clk}$):** $10\text{ MHz}$ square wave driving the comparator latch.
* **Simulation Time:** $2.2\text{ ms}$ (Enough to capture just over 2 full cycles of the $1\text{ kHz}$ input to account for startup transients).
* **Data Extraction:** The output of the comparator was probed, and LTspice's `.export` directive was used to write the digital state at every $100\text{ ns}$ interval to `pdm_output.txt`.

### Translating to the Digital Domain
The raw `pdm_output.txt` file exported from this simulation contains floating-point time data and voltage levels (e.g., $5.0\text{V}$ and $0.0\text{V}$). This file is passed to the Python scripts in the `3_Signal_Processing_Python` folder to be cleaned into pure binary `1`s and `0`s before being injected into the Vivado Verilog simulation.
